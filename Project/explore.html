Planets of Our Solar System

Eight incredible worlds orbiting our Sun

Mercury

The smallest and closest planet to the Sun, with extreme temperature variations and a heavily cratered surface.

  • Distance from Sun: 57.9 million km
  • Day Length: 59 Earth days
  • Year Length: 88 Earth days
Venus

The hottest planet in our solar system, with a thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid clouds.

  • Distance from Sun: 108.2 million km
  • Day Length: 243 Earth days
  • Year Length: 225 Earth days
Earth

Our home planet, the only known world to harbor life, with vast oceans and a protective atmosphere.

  • Distance from Sun: 149.6 million km
  • Day Length: 24 hours
  • Moons: 1 (Luna)
Mars

The Red Planet, featuring the largest volcano and canyon in the solar system, and potential for past life.

  • Distance from Sun: 227.9 million km
  • Day Length: 24.6 hours
  • Moons: 2 (Phobos, Deimos)
Jupiter

The largest planet, a gas giant with the famous Great Red Spot storm and 95 known moons.

  • Distance from Sun: 778.5 million km
  • Day Length: 10 hours
  • Moons: 95 known
Saturn

Famous for its spectacular ring system made of ice and rock particles, with 146 confirmed moons.

  • Distance from Sun: 1.4 billion km
  • Day Length: 10.7 hours
  • Moons: 146 known
Uranus

An ice giant that rotates on its side, giving it extreme seasonal variations and a unique blue-green color.

  • Distance from Sun: 2.9 billion km
  • Day Length: 17 hours
  • Moons: 27 known
Neptune

The windiest planet in our solar system, with supersonic winds and a deep blue color from methane atmosphere.

  • Distance from Sun: 4.5 billion km
  • Day Length: 16 hours
  • Moons: 16 known

Galaxies

Vast collections of billions of stars, gas, and dark matter

The Milky Way

Our home galaxy, a barred spiral galaxy containing 200-400 billion stars, including our Sun. It spans about 100,000 light-years in diameter.

Spiral Galaxy
Andromeda Galaxy

The nearest major galaxy to the Milky Way, located 2.5 million light-years away. It contains approximately 1 trillion stars and is on a collision course with our galaxy.

Spiral Galaxy
Sombrero Galaxy

A peculiar galaxy with a bright nucleus and large central bulge, resembling a Mexican hat. Located 28 million light-years away in the constellation Virgo.

Spiral Galaxy

Nebulae

Stellar nurseries and the remnants of dying stars

Orion Nebula

One of the brightest nebulae visible to the naked eye, located 1,344 light-years away. A stellar nursery where thousands of stars are being born.

Emission Nebula
Crab Nebula

The remnant of a supernova explosion observed in 1054 AD. Located 6,500 light-years away, it contains a pulsar spinning 30 times per second.

Supernova Remnant
Eagle Nebula

Home to the famous "Pillars of Creation," towering columns of gas and dust where new stars are forming. Located 7,000 light-years from Earth.

Star-Forming Region

Black Holes

The most mysterious objects in the universe

Supermassive Black Holes

Found at the centers of most galaxies, these giants contain millions to billions of solar masses. Our Milky Way's black hole, Sagittarius A*, has a mass of 4 million suns. Their immense gravity shapes entire galaxies.

Mass: Millions of Suns Galaxy Centers
Stellar Black Holes

Formed when massive stars collapse at the end of their lives. These black holes typically have masses 5-100 times that of our Sun. Nothing, not even light, can escape their powerful gravitational pull beyond the event horizon.

Mass: 5-100 Suns Stellar Remnants

The most common type of star, small and cool. They burn fuel slowly and can last for trillions of years.
